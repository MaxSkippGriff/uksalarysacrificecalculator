How it works

How salary sacrifice works on a £{{ "{:,}".format(salary) }} salary

With a £{{ "{:,}".format(salary) }} gross salary, a £{{ "{:,}".format(sacrifice) }} pension sacrifice reduces your taxable salary to £{{ "{:,.0f}".format(salary - sacrifice) }}. You pay income tax and National Insurance on the lower figure, not the original £{{ "{:,}".format(salary) }}.

{% if salary <= 50270 %}

At £{{ "{:,}".format(salary) }}, your salary sits within the basic-rate (20%) income tax band. Each £1,000 sacrificed saves £200 in income tax, plus £80 in employee NI (at the 8% rate below £50,270) — a combined saving of £280 per £1,000 sacrificed.

{% elif salary <= 100000 %}

At £{{ "{:,}".format(salary) }}, some or all of your sacrifice falls within the higher-rate (40%) income tax band. Higher-rate sacrifice saves 40p in income tax per £1 — significantly more efficient than basic-rate relief. {% if salary <= 50270 + sacrifice %} Part of the sacrifice reduces salary from within the basic-rate band (saving 20p per £1 in tax) and part from the higher-rate band (saving 40p per £1). {% endif %} Employee NI above £50,270 is only 2%, so the NI saving is smaller on that portion of salary.

{% else %}

At £{{ "{:,}".format(salary) }}, your salary exceeds £100,000 and the Personal Allowance begins to taper. Salary sacrifice can be particularly valuable here: reducing your adjusted net income below £100,000 can restore some or all of the £12,570 Personal Allowance, creating an effective marginal rate that can exceed 60% on income in the £100,000–£125,140 range.

{% endif %}

The £{{ "{:,}".format(sacrifice) }} goes into your pension. You receive the full £{{ "{:,}".format(sacrifice) }} pension contribution, but your take-home pay only falls by £{{ "{:,.0f}".format(calc.net_monthly_cost * 12) }} per year (£{{ "{:,.0f}".format(calc.net_monthly_cost) }}/month) — the difference is the tax and NI you no longer pay.
